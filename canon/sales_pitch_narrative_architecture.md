# The Core Insight

The bottleneck in B2B software sales has moved. For decades, the hard question was whether you could build the product. That constraint is largely solved. The new constraint is downstream: can you make the right buyer understand it, want it, commit to it, and say yes without the founder in the room?

Most founders treat conversion failure as a late-stage problem — a closing technique issue, a pricing issue, a follow-up issue. The real cause is earlier and structural: the buying path is missing. Not missing in the sense that nobody has thought about it. Missing in the sense that it has never been made explicit, transferable, and buyer-tested.

This is the thesis the deck exists to prove.

# The Three Failure Modes

Founders hit three distinct conversion failure modes depending on where they are in their commercial journey. These are not versions of the same problem — they are different manifestations of the same missing thing.

## Failure Mode 1 — Nobody self-selects (T1)

**What founders observe:** Calls happen but close rate is low. People seem interested but don't move. The feedback is false positive — nobody says the pitch is too broad. They say "interesting, let me think about it."

**What's actually happening:** The founder is running 3–5 simultaneous market bets — healthcare AND finance AND mid-market AND two verticals they met at a conference. Every surface has to speak to everyone, which means it resonates with no one. Each buyer feels like this *might* be for them, not that it *is* for them. Not enough to act. Not enough to say no. Just enough to stay warm and never convert.

**Why founders don't fix it:** Narrowing to one segment feels like giving something up. It feels like a shrinking bet. It is actually the opposite — concentration produces signal; hedging produces noise that looks like signal.

**The deck line (S03, T1 "But"):** *"Pitches to five segments at once. Nobody reads it and thinks: this is for me."*

## Failure Mode 2 — The story doesn't travel (T2)

**What founders observe:** Calls go well. Deck lands in the inbox. Then silence. The founder has to explain it again every time.

**What's actually happening:** The story only works when the founder voices it. The champion who received the deck can't carry it internally. They can't answer "why this vs what we use today" without the founder on the call. Materials require explanation — they don't work standalone.

**The deck line (S03, T2 "But"):** *"Deck lands in their inbox. Nobody replies without you explaining it."*

## Failure Mode 3 — The motion stops without the founder (T3)

**What founders observe:** Things work while they're watching. The moment they step back — hire a rep, bring in an agency, try to delegate — conversion drops. The system depends on the founder's presence.

**What's actually happening:** The motion was never installed as a transferable system. The rep improvises. The onboarding doc drifted. The pitch is different every time. There is no repeatable path that others can run.

**The deck line (S03, T3 "But"):** *"It runs fine while you're watching. The moment you step back, it stops converting."*

# The Root Cause — Dual-Sided

These three failure modes trace back to the same root, but it has two sides that must both be named.

**Seller side — fragmentation:** The buying path exists in fragments. Partly in the founder's head (and heads aren't built for holding complex, evolving commercial models). Partly in the rep's improvised version of the pitch. In last quarter's deck nobody updated. In onboarding docs that quietly drifted. Everyone has a piece. Nobody has the coherent, current, buyer-validated whole.

**Buyer side — inability to commit:** Because the path is fragmented and untested, prospects can't say yes. Or no. They default to ghosting, slow-playing, or "no decision" — not because they reject the offer, but because they can't make sense of what they're supposed to do. They can't commit. They can't move forward.

This is the Roelof Otten formulation from a discovery call (March 2026): *"The prospects cannot make sense of what it is and what they are supposed to do. They cannot say yes to it."*

The additional cost: because assumptions are never tested against real buyers, founders miss the rapid learning loop. Assumptions drift, turn out wrong, and compound quietly — while the founder stays blind to what's actually broken.

**The deck headline (S04):** *"The buying path is missing — so prospects can't say yes. Or no."*

**The deck pull-quote (S04):** *"Until it's explicit, transferable, and buyer-tested — prospects can't commit, and you can't learn fast enough to fix it."*

**The deck footer tag (S04):** *"Most 'no decisions' aren't rejections. They're just missing the clarity to commit."*

# The Positive Resolution

The resolution is not "write things down." That framing undersells what the engagement does and misrepresents the intervention.

The resolution has three components:

1. **The bet gets made.** A specific segment is chosen. The founder stops hedging. One market wedge is prioritised. This is a genuine strategic intervention — not documentation, but decision-making under discomfort. Session 2 of the Entry Product is explicitly a bet selection session. Founders often experience this as the hardest part of the engagement, because committing to one segment feels like risk. The reframe: staying broad is the higher-risk position, because it produces no actionable signal.
2. **The path gets locked.** 18 decisions — who buys, why they buy, what they use today, why that fails, what changes, how they commit — are locked in buyer language. Not internal operations language. Buyer language. This is the difference between "our sales motion is enterprise" (inside-out) and "how does the buyer approve purchases in this category" (outside-in).
3. **The motion gets installed.** The locked decisions are rendered into aligned surfaces — homepage, pitch deck, demo script, sales brief, objection map — and installed as a motion others can run. The test: could a first sales rep be onboarded from the Notion workspace alone, without the founder in the room?

**The deck headline (S06, statement slide):** *"Prospects can finally say yes."*

**The deck headline (S07, transitions unlocked):** *"The story travels and converts — without you in the room."*

# Key Narrative Decisions and Rationale

**Why the deck opens with symptoms, not the problem statement**

Founders don't recognise themselves in a problem statement. They recognise themselves in the experience. "Prospects go quiet. Reps can't close. Decks need voiceover." — these are things happening to them right now. The problem statement (buying path missing) lands harder after the symptom recognition, not before it.

**Why T1 failure mode is about too many bets, not about silence**

The other two "But" lines (T2, T3) describe conversion consequences. T1 describes an upstream cause — the bet was never made, so nobody self-selects. This is the only failure mode where the cause is visible before the call even happens. The founder can see it on their homepage right now. Naming the behaviour ("pitches to five segments") rather than the consequence ("gets ghosted") creates a more direct recognition moment.

**Why the root cause is dual-sided**

Previous versions of the deck overindexed on seller fragmentation — the knowledge is scattered, locked in heads, not written down. This is true but incomplete. The buyer-side consequence (can't say yes, defaults to no-decision) is what the founder actually experiences as pain. The diagnostic framing serves the [Product.Zone](http://Product.Zone) methodology; the buyer-consequence framing serves the sales conversation. Both need to be in the slide.

**Why "the bet gets made" is part of the resolution**

If the resolution is only "write things down and build surfaces," the deck undersells the intervention and attracts the wrong buyer — founders who want documentation help rather than founders who need a strategic intervention. The Entry Product's Session 2 is a bet selection session. That's harder and more valuable than most founders expect. The deck should communicate that the work involves *deciding*, not just *documenting*.

**Why the positive resolution slides say "finally"**

"Prospects can finally say yes." The word "finally" carries the emotional weight of everything that was stuck before. It's not just resolution — it's relief. That's the right register for a founder who has been watching qualified prospects ghost for months.

# What This Narrative Is Not

- Not a feature list. The deck never lists what [Product.Zone](http://Product.Zone) does as a set of capabilities. It describes the problem → root cause → resolution arc, and the capabilities emerge from that.
- Not a methodology explanation. The Buying Path OS and Revenue Loop appear as anchors, not as the subject of the pitch. The subject is the founder's situation.
- Not a case study presentation. Proof appears late (S11) and serves to validate the pattern, not to lead the conversation.

# Open Questions and Evolution

**D20 (open):** The positive resolution copy still says "Nothing changed about the product. Nothing changed about the market." This is wrong — the bet gets clearer, the segment gets chosen. Needs revision before Figma production of S06 statement slide.

**POV (open):** The "warm but never committing" failure mode — the specific texture of T1 — is not yet fully expressed in the deck. The current T1 line names the cause correctly but doesn't fully convey why it's so hard to diagnose (false positive feedback loop). May need elaboration in sales conversation, not necessarily in deck copy.
