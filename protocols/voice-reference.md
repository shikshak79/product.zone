# Voice & Language Reference
## Product.Zone + Hurozo

This is the canonical reference for written voice. All content skills that produce prose (hooks, post drafts, titles, descriptions) must apply these rules before outputting. If a section is uncertain, flag it for human rewrite rather than defaulting to safe AI language.

---

## The Standard

**Primary benchmark:** Matt Lakajev: direct, economically punchy, high energy. Doesn't soften. Names the problem without diplomatic packaging. Makes founders feel the cost of inaction.

**Secondary benchmark:** Emma Stratton: specific over vague, accessible, no jargon. Every sentence earns its place.

**The combination:** Lakajev's energy and economic directness, Stratton's specificity and accessibility. Not corporate. Not therapy. Not motivational. Direct, credible, and useful to a founder trying to solve a real problem.

---

## Sentence Structure

Write with rhythm. Longer sentences are fine, preferred even, if they stay scannable. The way you write in conversation is the model. Sentences have weight. They move. They don't pad.

**Good rhythm example:** "Founders keep refining their messaging. That's why conversion stays flat."

**What scannable means:** A reader skimming at pace can track the argument. Each sentence carries one idea. No sentence exists to bridge two other sentences. If it does, cut it.

Short punchy sentences work for emphasis and hooks. Not as the default register. Mixing lengths creates rhythm. Uniform short sentences read like a list. Uniform long sentences lose the reader.

---

## ESL Accessibility Rules

The reader may be European with English as a second language. The standard is not "simple". It is "no unnecessary barriers."

**Rule 1: No US-specific idioms that don't travel.** Terms like "drift", "boil the ocean", "table a discussion", "ballpark", "punt on it" are understood in US professional contexts but create friction or ambiguity elsewhere. Replace with plain equivalents.

**Rule 2: No insider terminology without explanation.** If a term requires a separate Google search to understand its meaning in context, it adds a barrier. Either explain inline or replace.

**Rule 3: Test this:** Could a German, Dutch, or French founder with solid but non-native English read this without stopping? If the answer is uncertain, simplify the term. Not the sentence. The term.

**Rule 4: Contractions are fine.** "It's", "you're", "they've": these are natural and accessible. Formal constructions ("it is", "you are") read as stiff and impersonal.

## AI Tells to Eliminate

These patterns must be removed from every draft before it is shown. They signal AI authorship immediately to a trained reader and erode credibility.

### Negative parallelisms
The most prominent AI tell. Constructions of the form "It's not X, it's Y" — whether written as one sentence or split across two.

The split-sentence version is equally LLM: "Founders don't have a copy problem. They have a structure problem." Same pattern, different punctuation. Still cuts.

Examples to cut:
- "It's not a content problem. It's a trust problem."
- "This isn't about tactics. It's about identity."
- "It's not what you say. It's how you say it."
- "Founders don't have a copy problem. They have a structure problem."
- "You don't need more content. You need better conversations."

These feel dramatic but deliver nothing. Replace with a direct statement of what is actually true.

**Wrong:** "Founders don't have a copy problem. They have a structure problem."
**Right:** "The copy isn't the issue. I've never run a sprint where the headline was what killed conversion."

The right version says the same thing but from a grounded position. It doesn't just assert the reframe, it earns it.

### Compulsive summaries
Restating what was just said at the end of a section.
- "In summary..."
- "Overall..."
- "In conclusion..."
- "To sum up..."
- "Ultimately..."

Cut entirely. If the point was made, it doesn't need to be announced as made.

### Hollow transitions
Overused connective tissue that adds no meaning:
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "It's important to understand that..."
- "This highlights the fact that..."
- "Notably..."
- "Interestingly..."

Cut the framing. Start with the thing itself.

### Vague attribution (weasel wording)
- "Many experts believe..."
- "Research suggests..."
- "Some argue..."
- "Studies show..."

If you have a specific source, name it. If you don't, make the claim as your own opinion or drop it.

### Inflated verb constructions
AI prefers elaborate over simple:
- "serves as" → is
- "functions as" → is
- "offers" → has
- "features" → has
- "ventured into" → entered
- "leverages" → uses
- "utilizes" → uses

Replace with the simple verb.

### The rule of three
AI defaults to grouping everything in threes. "Innovative, transformative, and groundbreaking." "Fast, simple, and effective." One strong specific word beats three stacked adjectives. If you're listing three things, ask whether two would be stronger.

### Ending sentences with -ing analysis
Tacking on a present participle that adds empty interpretation:
- "...improving convenience."
- "...signaling a shift in the market."
- "...highlighting the importance of trust."

These feel like analysis but deliver nothing. Cut the -ing clause. If the interpretation is worth making, make it as a sentence.

### Em dashes: never
Em dashes are one of the most recognisable AI writing tells. Do not use them. Not one. Not for emphasis, not for asides, not for rhythm.

Replace with:
- A comma, if the clause connects naturally
- A period, if the thought can stand alone
- Parentheses, if it is a genuine aside
- A colon, if you are introducing something

"Founders keep refining their messaging — that's why conversion stays flat." (wrong) → "Founders keep refining their messaging. That's why conversion stays flat."

### Excessive bolding
Bolding key terms like every sentence is a textbook entry. Bold nothing, or bold one thing per post if it is genuinely the hinge of the argument.

### Promotional inflation
Describing things as "fascinating", "remarkable", "groundbreaking", "transformative", "revolutionary", "game-changing" without evidence. These words raise the reader's skepticism. Replace with the specific outcome or result.

## Point of View

Write from "I", not from "most founders" or "many businesses."

Observational statements about what founders do, believe, or struggle with hang in the air unless backed by credible proof: a named study, a cited survey, a specific data set. Without that, "most founders do X" is an unearned claim that reads like AI filling in plausible-sounding context.

The alternative is always stronger: write from what you have actually seen, done, or measured.

**Wrong:** "Most founders optimise headlines without knowing which questions buyers need answered."
**Right:** "Every sprint I run starts the same way. The founder has spent weeks on copy. The structure underneath it is broken."

If a pattern is real, you have seen it in real clients. Name that: "Every founder I've worked with this quarter..." or "In the last five sprints..." That is credible. "Most founders" is not.

**When third-party claims are allowed:**
- Named research with a source you can cite ("69 out of 80 founders in our category research used the word 'pitch'")
- A client quote or outcome you have permission to reference
- An observation you can back with your own data from sprints or audits

If you cannot point to the specific source, write from "I" instead.

## Tone Rules

**Direct, not aggressive.** Name the problem plainly. Don't soften it with "might" or "could" when you mean "is". But don't perform anger. The energy comes from conviction, not volume.

**Economic, not motivational.** The reader is a founder with a revenue problem. The frame is always: this costs you money, or this makes you money. Not: this will transform your mindset.

**Specific, not universal.** "Founders with 10–60 users who can't explain why conversion is inconsistent" is better than "any business owner". Specificity earns trust. Vagueness loses it.

**Own the opinion.** "I think" and "in my view" are fine occasionally. But don't hedge every claim. If you believe it, say it without the wrapper. The wrapper is what makes posts sound like AI.

**Never inspirational without evidence.** Statements like "You still have time" or "The best is yet to come" read as empty without concrete grounding. Connect every motivational beat to a specific action or outcome.

## Analogy Style

Analogies should come from the founder's operational world, not consumer life.

**Good territory:** product builds, sales conversations, hiring, onboarding, customer calls, sprint processes, investor decks, pricing decisions.

**Avoid:** gym metaphors (overused on LinkedIn), Tinder comparisons (too casual for the Product.Zone positioning), sports (US-centric, not universal).

The analogy should make the founder self-diagnose: "Am I in this situation?" Not just: "Yes, that's a true statement about the world."

**The oil change test:** "Oil change, stay in your car" works because it tells you what happens (your car gets fixed), how long it takes (you stay in your car), and signals the value without jargon. Every analogy should pass this test: specific, fast, no explanation overhead.

## The Refinement Process

Content goes through this sequence before it is final. Each pass has a specific job:

1. **Rough draft:** get the substance down. Voice doesn't matter yet.
2. **Remove jargon:** replace positioning/consulting/AI industry terms with plain language.
3. **Remove LLM-speak:** apply the AI tells list above. Cut every negative parallelism, hollow transition, vague attribution.
4. **ESL test:** read for terms that don't travel. Replace anything that needs a separate explanation.
5. **Final polish:** rhythm, sentence length, energy. Does it sound like you?

Expect 5–10 cycles on high-stakes content. Not because the draft is bad. Because voice takes iteration.

---

## Diagnostic Questions (run before any content is finalised)

1. Does every sentence carry one clear idea?
2. Is there a negative parallelism anywhere? ("It's not X, it's Y") → cut it.
3. Is there a compulsive summary? ("In summary", "Ultimately") → cut it.
4. Are there any vague attributions? ("Research suggests") → name it or own it.
5. Would a Dutch founder with solid English pause on any term? → replace it.
6. Does the hook make someone stop mid-scroll, or does it announce itself? Hooks don't announce. They interrupt.
7. Is the economic cost or gain visible? → if not, add it.
8. Does it sound like it came from a person who knows what they're talking about, or from a tool? → if the latter, rewrite.
